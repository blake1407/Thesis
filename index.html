Research Question
Did implicit stereotypes about Muslim/Arab and Israeli/Jewish groups shift in political discourse following the Hamas attack on October 7, 2023?
Stereotype categories — drawn from Kurdi et al. (2019) — span warmth, coldness, competence, and incompetence, applied across group pairs using cosine-similarity-based WEAT association scores (Caliskan et al., 2017).

WEAT Analysis
SC-WEAT and double-category WEAT quantify implicit stereotypes by comparing cosine similarities between target groups and SCM attribute pairs (Warm/Cold, Competence/Incompetence) using permutation testing on BERT embeddings.
⚠ IDF was not mentioned by any influencer in the "Before" period (10/07/22–10/06/23), so SC-WEAT could not be performed for this group pre-conflict. In the "After" period, Hamas was discussed substantially more (n = 375 tweets) compared to IDF (n = 17 tweets).

Before Oct 7 (Tables 4 & 5): No significant effects across Israeli, Jewish, Arabic, Muslim groups. IDF and Hamas absent from pre-conflict corpus entirely.

After Oct 7 (Tables 6 & 7): All six groups showed significant negative D-scores on both SCM dimensions — cold and incompetent stereotypes applied indiscriminately across all groups in conflict discourse.

Change over time (Tables 8–11): Double-category WEAT comparing before vs. after embeddings reveals significant shifts — e.g. Israeli group became less warm (D = −0.507 on Cold dimension) while Arabic group became significantly colder (D = +1.080). All effects p = .001.

Stage 06 · Results interpretation
Discussion
Interpretation of SC-WEAT findings, bipartisan differences in stereotype language, limitations of the study, and directions for future research.

3.1 Single-Category WEAT — Key Findings

BERT embeddings found no statistical significance in the effect sizes of all social groups and SCM attribute combinations in the "Before" period. However, in the "After" period, SC-WEAT using BERT vectors revealed highly significant associations of all six target groups with low warmth and low competence:

Israeli low warmth (D = −0.242; p = .005) and low competence (D = −0.346; p = .004) · IDF low warmth (D = −0.211; p = .012) and low competence (D = −0.339; p = .006) · Jewish low warmth (D = −0.261; p = .003) and low competence (D = −0.348; p = .002) · Arabic low warmth (D = −0.228; p = .006) and low competence (D = −0.340; p = .005) · Hamas low warmth (D = −0.208; p = .01) and low competence (D = −0.319; p = .007) · Muslim low warmth (D = −0.294; p < .001) and low competence (D = −0.360; p = .005).

The study revealed significant, negative consequences of the conflict narrative on social media regarding stereotype and implicit association of the implicated social groups. While there were bipartisan differences in advocacy for international policies regarding the conflict in the Gaza Strip, support and non-support of influencers for Israeli and/or Jewish or Arabic and/or Muslim people were both accompanied by implicit association with low warmth and low competence after the start of the war.

However, it is unclear whether these associations were a natural consequence of the turmoil and distress around wartime, or reflected the influencers' implicit opinions. It is also important to note that these associations cannot be generalized to the larger population in the U.S. that used X around the time of the conflict, as readers' perception or action beyond seeing such tweets are beyond the scope of the study.

Bipartisan Differences

Bipartisan differences in how Israeli and/or Jewish, Arabic and/or Muslim, IDF, and Hamas were described were consistent with the broad stance of the political parties in America. As the majority of right-leaning adults were surveyed to be more sympathetic towards Israel and its objectives compared to that of Palestinians (AP-NORC Center for Public Affairs Research, 2024), the stereotypical language of right-leaning influencers became consistently less critical compared to that of left-leaning influencers after the start of the conflict in the Competence dimension.

However, as the conflict progresses, it is difficult to explain how the significant difference in negativity in discussing the IDF compared to Hamas for right-leaning influencers arises. Within the timeline of the conflict, negative sentiment can be diversely utilized in discussions of grief, humanitarian crises, national security concerns, and in critiques of the opposing party's opinions.

In contrast, left-leaning influencers were observed to have a significant difference in their language when discussing the IDF and Hamas in the Competence dimension, where IDF was explicitly regarded as both more competent and incompetent after the start of the conflict. These polarizing opinions can be due to the internal division of the left wing regarding the stance of the U.S. on the conflict (Kamarck et al., 2024).

Since Hamas was discussed more frequently by the influencers in the study (nHamas = 375 vs. nIDF = 17), future research should examine the bipartisan differences in how the IDF and Hamas were portrayed with a different group of political influencers.

Limitations & Future Directions

Small corpora & unequal mentions

The study used a limited number of influencers and the natural noise of social data from X, with unequal mentions of all social groups implicated in the conflict.

Platform algorithmic bias

Textual data was heavily reliant on results displayed by X's Advanced Search tools and X's own algorithmic bias per platform policy.

Computational language lag

Computational methods always lag behind the development of human natural language. More sophisticated measures for topical categorization of tweets should be considered in future work.

Cross-platform generalizability

Future research should explore whether these implicit associations were present equally across different social media platforms throughout the timeline of the conflict.

Longitudinal scope

More in-depth, extended longitudinal studies of the social groups implicated would offer insights into how stereotype content fluctuates during high and low-tension intergroup contact.

IDF / Hamas imbalance

IDF was absent from the pre-conflict corpus entirely, and mentioned only 17 times post-conflict vs. 375 for Hamas. Future studies should use a different influencer sample to better balance these groups.
